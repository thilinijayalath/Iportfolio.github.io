The Three P’s of Lesson Planning

  1. The Presentation Stage

    2. This is where the language is introduced, or ‘presented’ to the learners, usually by way of introducing a context or situation. For example, you could:

    • Tell or act out a short story or anecdote
    • Play a short audio clip
    • Show a clip from a movie or tv show
    • Show objects you’ve brought in (e.g. newspaper cuttings, plane tickets, hobby materials)

    The aim is to make sure students understand the context, and to get them thinking about it. You could elicit ideas or suggestions from students, get them talk to each other about what they know or think about the situation, etc. This also helps them start to remember the language and vocabulary they already know about the topic (or ‘activate the schemata’, if you want the fancy term for it).

  2. The Practice Stage

    3. The ‘practice’ stage is when students use the language in a controlled way. This stage is sometimes divided into two - a controlled practice and a freer practice. Again, among many things, you could get students to:

    • Drill sentences or sounds, chorally or individually
    • Substitution drill in pairs
    • Sentence matching activities
    • Gap fill exercises
    • Pair work asking and answering questions

    The aim of this stage is accuracy. Error correction is important in this stage, so make sure you monitor the students closely and take time to error correct immediately. For target language errors that seem to be common, a delayed error correction section after the activity would be useful.

  3. The Production Stage

    4. The ‘production’ stage is where the language is used in a more open way. Things like:

    • Role-plays
    • Communication tasks
    • Collaborative tasks
    • Discussion activities

    The focus of this stage is using the language as fluently and naturally as possible, as students would do outside of the classroom.



Advantages and Disadvantages of 3 P’s of Lesson Planning


Advantages

  • It’s easy to learn for new teachers
  • It’s very flexible
  • It’s easy to plan for, and has a logical progression
  • It works for most types of class, including larger classes
  • Most course books use this, or a similar, method to structure their lessons and chapters

Disadvantages

  • Research shows that it may not be the best way to teach / learn a language
  • Weaker learners may overuse the target language from the practice session, so it sounds unnatural
  • Learners may not be sure how to use target language in different contexts
  • It can be boring if used repeatedly for higher level students


The 3 Stages for Teaching Reading

  1. The Pre-Reading Stage

    2. Pre-reading activities are activities that helps students think about what they know about a topic and predict what they will read or hear.The purpose of pre-reading activities are to:

    • Establish a purpose for reading
    • Improve vocabulary so students can complete the reading task successfully
    • Predict what they are going
    • Establish what they know about a topic

  2. The While-Reading Stage

    3. While-Reading Activities are defined as activities that help students to focus on aspects of the text and to understand it better. The goal of these activities is to help learners to deal as they would deal with it as if the text was written in their first language.Followinhs are some of examples for While-Reading activities:

    • Identify topic sentences and the main idea of paragraphs.
    • Distinguish between general and specific ideas.
    • Identify the connectors to see how they link ideas within the text.

  3. The Post-Reading Stage

    4. Post-reading activities help students understand texts further, through critically analyzing what they have read. Some Post-Reading examples are:

    • Creative Discussions
    • Quiz Your Classmates
    • Finding Related News



Effective Lesson Planning


  1. Effective Teachers;
    • Know the development of the student
    • Use effective communication
    • Manage the teaching environment properly          
  2. A good teacher should have good;
    • Strategies – (good strategies)
    • Operation – (well operation)
    • Tactics – (good tactics)
  3. A good teacher should know better;
    • Strategic management.
    • Operational management
    • Tactics management.





Good Planning;

  • Keeps the teacher & students on track
  • Achieves the objectives
  • Help teachers to avoid “unpleasant” surprises
  • Provide the road map & visuals in a logical sequence                                    
  • Encourage reflection, refinement & improvement
  • Enhance student achievements


A Good Lesson


  1. Should have a format
    • Try to conceptualize the lesson
    • Minimize the number of letters
  2. Do a pre assessment
    • Know who are the audience
    • What modifications they need
    • How they want to learn?
    • What they already know?
  3. Know objectives
    • A description of what the student will be able to do after the session                                    
  4. Materials
    • Plan, Prepare, have on hand
    • Prepare your resources / materials before the session
  5. Warm up & introduction
    • Grab the student attention
    • Be creative
  6. Procedures & presentation
    • Provide a quick review of previous learning
    • Provide modeling of a new skill
  7. Learning activities
    • Creative play – Projects
    • Debates – Peer presenting
    • Game making – inquiry learning
  8. Practice (Applying what is learned)
  9. Closure
    • Leave student with an imprint of what the lesson covered
      • Student summarize the major concepts
      • Teacher recaps the main points
    • Evaluation
      • In class / homework assignment
      • Performance assessment
      • Portfolios
      • Journals
      • Informal assessment
      • Use of rubrics
    • Reflection
      • What went well in the lesson?
      • What problem did I experience?
      • Why we did this/ What can we do better in next time
